View or Edit Project

Use this screen to update the project header details, to write and update your project narrative and to manage and oversee your project. Here you can attach grants which have been awarded to the project, request feedback from colleagues on the project narrative and see the feedback that has been given. You can also attach files to the project, such as flyers or meeting minutes, to do lists, etc. so you can keep all of the information about the project in one place.

How do I ...

Edit project header
  1. Click Edit on the project header.
  2. Amend the Name, Start date, Completion date and Target amount (what the project will cost and which you need to raise to deliver the project) as required.
  3. Amend the Description. This is a succinct description of the project. Provide information about what you are aiming to achieve with this project and what impact it will have. Don’t worry about including everything in the description, as you can expand on your points in the further sections. Try to make a big but positive impact with the description. It should tell the story of what you are trying to achieve, emphasising the gains the school and the local community will make through the project and should encapsulate the core of the project so that you can use it as an elevator pitch when people in local businesses or other interested parties ask you about your project.
  4. Click Save Project.

Note: You can’t edit the Project Strength. This is calculated internally within Arro based on the recommended word count for your project narrative and is there to help you progress to completing your project.

Write or update a project narrative?
  • Click Edit on the Narrative tab.

    • You should include as much information as possible in your project narrative. Questions are provided for you to guide you through the detail of what you need to provide.

    Why do you need your project?

    When describing the need for your project, describe the problem your project is addressing. It may be that a lack of access to similar facilities is limiting the development of children within the area. What is the current situation with your existing facilities? What are you being prevented from achieving as an organisation, or for your pupils, as a result of not upgrading your facilities? This is one of the most important parts of your Project Narrative. You have to be able to prove to funders that there is a genuine need for your project. If there are any agendas your project can address then include it.

    What impact will your project have?

    While your project need describes the situation now, you also need to be able to show potential supporters what the future will look like once your project has been completed. What will be better as result of your project? Grant donors want to know what difference their money will make, so you need to be able to prove that your project will lead to a positive change.

    What supporting evidence do you have?
    You need to provide evidence that supports your project’s impact.
    Are there any studies highlighting improvements in development for children with access to your project? If you are addressing a specific agenda (for example: an ageing community) can you provide evidence that having access to your project will have a positive impact on health and wellbeing? Statistics are really powerful here. If you can link to studies which have already been done that quantify the impact of your project on health and wellbeing etc. then it will greatly improve your chances of acquiring funding.

    Have you done any research yourself? Have you consulted your stakeholders and the wider community to confirm that they support your project and that it would be used? Again, if you can provide statistical evidence based on survey results for example, it will go a long way to evidencing that your project is needed.

    Also, have any other similar projects been run by other schools or organisations? If you can find another school who has a similar project and can provide you with impact statistics that would be great. If there are lessons you can learn from other projects, make sure you are considering them.

    How does your project link to your school vision and goals?

    It is also worth discussing how your project links to your school’s overall vision and goals. If your vision is to become a regional centre of excellence for special needs education, then you should write about why your new project will help make that happen. Again, think about access for children, stakeholders and community members in the area. If you are the only school that can provide this project in the area, then it is important to include that.

    How do you plan to deliver, monitor and evaluate your project?

    Supporters want to be confident that your project will be delivered. You will need to provide some information about timescales, project delivery and how you are going to monitor whether your project is on or off track. If you are not asking a supporter for the whole amount you need for your project, then you will need to explain where the rest of the money is going to come from.

    How will you measure and report success?

    Supporters also want to know if your project has been a success. It is worth having plans in place for measuring the impact of your project. If you have said that you will see a specific improvement, how are you going to measure that improvement? What reporting processes are you going to put in place to ensure all stakeholders understand the impact that the project has made?

  • Click Done to save changes to your project narrative.
Monitor the project progress?
  • View the high level information about the project at the top of the screen, for example, the project name, funding target and what you have raised currently for the project.

    • Note: The amount raised does not include any grants that you have been awarded. The figure reflects purely the donations and any sponsorship that you have received that has been allocated to the project.

  • Project Strength is an internal measure of how close you are to being ready to market the project for donations through the community page. When you first create a project, strength will be around 5%. It is calculated internally within Arro based on the recommended word count for your project narrative and is there to help you progress to completing your project. Once your project has been approved by Pebble’s copywriter, this will show as 100% Pebble Approved and ready to market.
  • Sharing project with colleagues is a good way to invite collaboration on the project from your colleagues while it is in development. If you have requested feedback from your colleagues, you will see the feedback from your colleagues alongside the project narrative.
  • See what grants you have attached to the project, by clicking on the Grants tab.
  • Check what files you have attached to the project, using the Files tab.
  • If your project has been Pebble Approved, have you created a community page for the project? If not you will see the text at the top of the screen.

    • Once you create your community page, you can begin to tell people about the project and start seeing donations come in. You can share the link to the project community page on Facebook and on Twitter and on your school website.

Request feedback from colleagues?
  1. To gather feedback on the project before you take it to market, click Share with Colleagues
  2. Enter the Name and Email Address of the person you want to share the project details with and click Add Contact.

    3. Note: You can add a number of contacts to request feedback. When you click Add Contact, the list of recipients is updated below the Name and Email Address fields.

  3. Include a message to your colleague, for example, Please review and give feedback on the project details.
  4. Click Share Project.

    5. An email is sent to the contact you have chosen to share the project with. The email contains a link which they can click which takes them to a page where they can add their comments and you will be able to see these comments alongside the project details on the Narrative tab.

View and respond to feedback?

If you have shared your project with colleagues and requested feedback, you will be able to see their comments on the Narrative tab alongside the project details.

  • You can see who has left the comment, what the comment is, and how long ago it was left.
  • To reply, enter the reply text in the Reply To Your Contributors box and click Add Reply. The reply is added to the list of comments and an email is sent to the contributor to notify them you have replied.
Find and attach grants to the project?
  1. Select the Grants tab.
  2. To view the details for a grant already attached to the project, click the Grant Title.
  3. To add grants, click Search for Grants and follow the instructions on the Grants dashboard.
Apply for a grant attached to the project?
  1. Select the Grants tab.
  2. Click the Grant Title to display an outline of information about the grant.
  3. Click Read More.
  4. Follow the information provided in the What Next section of the grant information.
Record successful and unsuccessful grant applications?

There is currently no way to record if you have been successful or unsuccessful in your grant applications within Arro. However, we recommend that you create a document to contain this information and attach the document to the project. This will ensure that you keep track of which applications have been successful and which have not.

Attach files to the project?
  1. Select the Files tab.

    2. Any files that have already been added to the project are listed.

  2. To view the details of files that have been added, click the File Name.
  3. To add files, click + Add New. The Add File window opens.
    1. Click Choose a file, browse to select the file that you want to upload and click Open.
    2. Browse to select the file that you want to upload.
    3. Enter a memorable name for the file you are uploading. This makes it easy to identify when you read it in the list of files attached to the project.
    4. Click Upload.
      5. Is the file taking a long time to upload?

      There may be browser issues when uploading files to projects in IE browsers. If you find an upload is taking a long time then try uploading using Chrome or Firefox to see if this fixes the issue.

Edit a community page?

Change the community page URL?

You can give your community page a more easily recognisable URL to help people find it. The community page will still be accessible from its current link.

Note: You can only change this once, so choose carefully.

  1. Click Edit Link.
  2. Enter the Joinos Community Page link in the box. provided.

    3. The link you enter will be appended to https://give.pebblecloud.net.

  3. Click Change Link.
Share the project community page on Twitter?
  1. Click Twitter.
  2. Log on to your school Twitter account to Authorise Pebble School Tweeter to tweet on your behalf.
Share the project community page on Facebook?
  1. Click Facebook.
  2. Logon to your school Facebook account to share the community page on Facebook.
